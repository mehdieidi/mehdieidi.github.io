# Stop Building a Second Brain. Start Solving Real Problems.

Every few months I come across another article or video promising that a **"second brain"** will change the way I learn. The advice is usually familiar: capture everything, link everything, build a personal Wikipedia, and trust that your future self will thank you.

I have a problem with that idea.

Not because note-taking is useless—but because I think we've quietly forgotten to ask a much more fundamental question:

> **Why should I write this note at all?**

## The Question Nobody Starts With

Imagine you're learning recursion, Docker, SQL, or Linux.

Should you create notes explaining what they are?

My answer is usually **no**.

There are already excellent books, documentation, university lectures, blog posts, and Wikipedia articles. Any explanation I write is almost certainly worse than the original.

If my note merely duplicates documentation, what have I actually gained?

The internet does not have an information shortage. Quite the opposite.

The scarce resources are:

- understanding,
- experience,
- judgment,
- decisions,
- and context.

Those are the things Google cannot search for on my behalf.

## Don't Compete With Documentation

Many people unintentionally turn Obsidian into a miniature encyclopedia.

They write notes like:

- "What is Docker?"
- "How recursion works."
- "What is JSON?"
- "How Git branches work."

The problem isn't that these notes are incorrect.

The problem is that they are **replaceable**.

If deleting a note loses nothing that couldn't be recovered in thirty seconds from official documentation, that note has very little long-term value.

## What Makes a Note Worth Keeping?

A note becomes valuable when it captures something that only you can produce.

For example:

> "I finally understood recursion when I stopped thinking about a function calling itself and started thinking about reducing a problem until the answer became trivial."

Or:

> "I kept confusing Docker images and containers. The analogy that finally clicked was: image = class, container = object."

These aren't facts.

They're evidence of understanding.

Even better are notes like:

> "We chose PostgreSQL over MongoDB because our reporting requirements favored relational queries."

Or:

> "This production bug happened because our reverse proxy stripped the `Authorization` header."

Those notes preserve expensive thinking that would otherwise need to be repeated.

## The Real Test

I now use a simple mental filter before creating a note:

> **If I deleted this note tomorrow, what knowledge would disappear from the world?**

If the answer is:

*"Nothing. It's already in the official docs."*

I probably don't need the note.

If the answer is:

*"I'd lose the reasoning behind a decision, a hard-earned lesson, or the explanation that finally made something click."*

Now the note has earned its place.

## The Myth of Capturing Everything

The productivity world often encourages us to capture everything because storage is cheap.

But storage isn't the cost.

Attention is.

Every note becomes another object to organize, maintain, review, search, and mentally filter.

The goal should not be the largest vault.

It should be the highest signal-to-noise ratio.

Perhaps only five percent of what we learn deserves to become a permanent note.

The other ninety-five percent can safely remain in books, documentation, papers, or our memory.

## Notes Are Not the Product

Here's a thought experiment.

Suppose every note you've ever written disappeared tomorrow.

Would your career collapse?

Probably not.

Now suppose every project you've ever completed disappeared.

That would be catastrophic.

Projects create capability.

Notes merely support capability.

The note is rarely the destination.

It is a tool that occasionally makes future work easier.

## Sometimes the Note Isn't for Future You

One important exception deserves mentioning.

Writing itself is a powerful thinking tool.

Richard Feynman was famous for exposing gaps in his own understanding by trying to explain concepts clearly. Many writers, researchers, and engineers experience the same phenomenon: attempting to write often reveals what they don't yet understand.

In those cases, the value isn't necessarily in rereading the note years later.

The value happened during the act of writing.

The note may never be opened again—and that's perfectly fine.

## My Philosophy

I don't want a personal Wikipedia.

I don't want a second brain.

I want a notebook that contains things that would actually be expensive to lose.

That usually means:

- design decisions,
- lessons learned,
- failed experiments,
- surprising observations,
- reusable mental models,
- project-specific knowledge,
- and insights that changed how I think.

Everything else already has a better home.

## A Better Question

Instead of asking:

> "Should I create a note about recursion?"

Ask:

> **"What would future me lose if this note never existed?"**

If the answer is "nothing important," don't write it.

Good note-taking is not about capturing more.

It's about capturing what cannot be easily recreated.

## References

1. Richard P. Feynman, *Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman!* (1985). The book illustrates Feynman's habit of testing understanding by explaining ideas clearly.
2. Peter C. Brown, Henry L. Roediger III, & Mark A. McDaniel, *Make It Stick: The Science of Successful Learning* (Harvard University Press, 2014). Emphasizes retrieval, elaboration, and active processing over passive copying.
3. John Sweller, "Cognitive Load During Problem Solving: Effects on Learning," *Cognitive Science*, 12(2), 1988. Introduced Cognitive Load Theory and explains why indiscriminate information accumulation is not inherently beneficial.
4. Tiago Forte, *Building a Second Brain* (Atria Books, 2022). Although I disagree with applying its ideas universally, it is one of the most influential modern books on personal knowledge management and is worth reading critically.
5. Andy Matuschak, "Evergreen notes." https://notes.andymatuschak.org/ — A thoughtful perspective on note-making that emphasizes evolving ideas rather than static summaries.
